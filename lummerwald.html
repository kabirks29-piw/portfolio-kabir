Overview

This project applies static and dynamic simulation techniques using SIMBA software to optimise the biological wastewater treatment process at the Lummerwald WWTP, a facility serving a population equivalent (PE) of 500,000 inhabitants. The plant treats a daily wastewater inflow of 60,000 m³/day (120 L/IV/d) with influent characteristics of COD = 650 mg/L, KN = 65 mg/L, and P = 10 mg/L.

Treated effluent must comply with strict limit values of COD ≤ 75 mg/L, Ntot ≤ 13 mg/L, and SNH ≤ 10 mg/L year-round. The central engineering challenge is that the existing plant configuration fails to meet effluent standards during winter operation at 8°C — specifically, nitrification and denitrification processes break down due to critically low autotrophic biomass (XA = 5 g COD/m³ at 8°C versus 50 g COD/m³ at 15°C).

The study evaluates three configurations: the existing process (baseline diagnosis), Alternative 1 (sludge recycle modification), and Alternative 2 (step-feed cascade with/without relay-and-switch aeration control), all simulated at three seasonal temperatures.

Methodology

Existing Process Diagnosis: SIMBA CSTR analysis at 8°C revealed the root cause of winter failure: autotrophic biomass (XA) collapses from ~50 g COD/m³ at 15°C to just 5 g COD/m³ at 8°C. With almost no XA present, effluent SNH at 8°C reaches 33 mg/L against a limit of 10 mg/L. The existing band filter at 2,500 m³/day is removing too much sludge, preventing biomass accumulation.

Alternative 1 — Modified Existing: Reducing excess sludge withdrawal from 2,500 m³/day to 1,500 m³/day. XA in the aerobic zone recovers from 5 to 86 g COD/m³ at 8°C, achieving full compliance at all temperatures. Sludge treatment cost drops ~40%.

Figure 2 — Alternative 1: CSTR Analysis at 8°C
Restored nitrification/denitrification after sludge withdrawal reduction to 1,500 m³/day

Alternative 2a — Step Feed Configuration: Splits activated sludge volume into three equal anoxic/aerobic stage pairs with influent introduced at three points. Creates multiple denitrification opportunities, reducing Ntot by a further 1–2 mg/L vs. Alternative 1 at all temperatures.

Alternative 2b — Step Feed + Relay & Switch Control: A relay-and-switch control block added to the first nitrification tank's aeration, triggered by effluent SNO concentration (switch-on at SNO = 4.5 mg/L). Automatically reduces aeration when adequate biological activity is occurring, eliminating constant over-aeration at 15°C and 20°C.

  • Root cause of winter failure is a control parameter issue, not a design flaw: excess sludge withdrawal at 2,500 m³/day prevents sufficient autotrophic biomass accumulation for nitrification at 8°C.
  • Reducing withdrawal to 1,500 m³/day restores compliance with zero physical plant changes, reducing sludge treatment costs by ~40%.
  • Step-feed (Alt-2a) provides superior nitrogen removal — Ntot from 10–11 to 7–10 mg/L — at the cost of higher capital investment for additional anoxic tanks.
  • Alternative 2b (Step Feed + Relay/Switch) is the optimal solution: 13.5% aeration cost reduction (saving €89,193/year) with maintained effluent quality.
  • Dynamic simulation confirms all three alternatives maintain effluent compliance under variable inflow conditions.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates the power of SIMBA-based static and dynamic simulation in diagnosing and resolving a real-world WWTP performance failure. The relay-and-switch aeration control (Alternative 2b) is the optimal solution overall: it achieves the same nitrogen removal performance as step-feed while cutting annual aeration costs by 13.5% by automatically reducing aeration when effluent SNO confirms adequate biological activity.

Further performance improvements recommended include anaerobic digestion of excess sludge to generate biogas revenue, optimising sludge recycle pipeline diameters, and regular pump maintenance to sustain the 1,500 m³/day control set point. The SIMBA model's ASM3 limitation (no phosphorus removal module) means P removal would require additional investigation.
