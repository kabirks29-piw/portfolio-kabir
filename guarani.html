Overview

The Guarani Aquifer System (GAS) is one of the largest freshwater aquifer systems on Earth, covering approximately 1 million km² across four South American nations: Brazil (68%), Argentina (21%), Paraguay (8%), and Uruguay (3%). Estimated to hold between 37,000 and 40,000 km³ of water, the GAS supplies approximately 15 million people and represents a critical shared resource for agriculture, industry, and domestic water supply across the Southern Cone.

This academic poster investigates the transboundary governance framework developed to manage the GAS sustainably — focusing on the Guarani Aquifer System Project (PSAG), a landmark multi-country initiative funded by the Global Environment Facility (GEF) and the World Bank, coordinated through the Organisation of American States (OAS). The poster traces the full diplomatic and institutional history of PSAG from its 2000 inception through its 2003 operational launch, analyses its multi-layered governance architecture, and evaluates the key lessons for international water law and transboundary groundwater management.

Content & Analysis

The GAS is a sedimentary aquifer of Triassic-Jurassic origin, formed in the Paraná and Chaco-Paraná basins. It exists as both confined (artesian, under pressure from overlying basalt formations) and unconfined zones. Groundwater temperatures in artesian zones can reach 33–65°C, making the GAS a potential geothermal energy resource.

Key Findings

  • Technical and institutional cooperation was achieved across four countries with significantly different legal, administrative, and political contexts — producing joint technical assessments, shared monitoring networks, and common management domain definitions.
  • Localised communication strategies were identified as critical: approaches effective in Brazil's densely monitored states were often ineffective in Paraguay's or Uruguay's rural communities, highlighting limits of purely top-down frameworks.
  • The Project Executive Unit (PEU) emerged as the institutional lynchpin: its ability to translate strategic decisions from the SPSC into practical cross-border activities was the single most operationally critical factor.
  • Continuous financial support is a structural vulnerability: long-term sustainable management requires embedding financial commitments into national water governance budgets rather than relying on project-based international funding cycles.
  • PSAG advances international water law principles — demonstrating that transboundary groundwater systems can be governed under a framework respecting national sovereignty while mandating operational cooperation, applicable to 600+ transboundary aquifers globally.

Conclusion

The Guarani Aquifer System Project represents one of the most comprehensive and diplomatically complex transboundary groundwater governance initiatives in history. PSAG demonstrates that transboundary aquifer governance is feasible even across multiple sovereign states with different legal traditions, provided that: (a) a neutral multilateral coordinating body (OAS) provides the diplomatic platform; (b) international financial mechanisms (GEF/World Bank) provide the initial catalytic funding; and (c) a competent, adequately resourced Project Executive Unit handles day-to-day cross-border coordination.

The GAS case highlights the academic community's underappreciated contribution to transboundary water management: scientific research on aquifer recharge zones, contamination risks, and groundwater flow patterns provided the shared technical knowledge base without which diplomatic cooperation would have been impossible. International water law must evolve to explicitly address groundwater systems — the 2008 ILC Draft Articles on the Law of Transboundary Aquifers, informed in part by the GAS experience, represent the first step toward such a framework.
