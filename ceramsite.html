Ceramsite Recovery from Dewatered Sludge

Techno-Economic Feasibility Study: Rotary Kiln Sintering of Municipal Sludge and MSWI Fly Ash for Ceramsite Production vs. Biogas Generation

Overview

This study investigates the technical feasibility and economic viability of producing ceramsite — a lightweight, porous, high-strength aggregate — from municipal sewage sludge combined with municipal solid waste incineration (MSWI) fly ash, using a rotary kiln sintering process. The work was conducted as part of the Innovative Bioprocesses for Wastewater/Waste Valorization module at Leibniz Universität Hannover's ISAH.

The core problem addressed is the growing volume of municipal sludge generated by wastewater treatment plants globally — and specifically in rapidly urbanising China, where 215.2 million tonnes of municipal solid waste were generated in 2017 alone, with 40% incinerated, producing approximately 300,000 tonnes of fly ash annually. Both sewage sludge and MSWI fly ash are problematic waste streams: the former contains heavy metals that limit conventional reuse pathways, and the latter is classified as hazardous waste in China due to high heavy metal and dioxin content.

The study demonstrates that sintering a blended feedstock of sewage sludge and MSWI fly ash at 1,080°C for 8 minutes in a rotary kiln produces sludge ceramsite with comparable properties to conventional clay ceramsite — lightweight, waterproof, hard, and non-toxic — while simultaneously immobilising heavy metals within the ceramic matrix.

Methodology

The chemical composition of dried sewage sludge, MSWI fly ash, and reference clay was determined by X-ray fluorescence spectrometry. Results were compared against Riley's (1950) criteria for lightweight aggregate production. The optimal bulk ratio of sewage sludge to MSWI fly ash was determined to be 8:2.

The ceramsite production process comprises five sequential stages modelled in STAN: (1) Sludge Dewatering — 3,950 t/yr input, 3,239 t water removed; (2) Sludge Drying — moisture reduced to 10–15%; (3) Batching & Granulation — 8:2 sludge-to-fly-ash blend; (4) Sintering & Curing at 1,080°C for 8 minutes; (5) Dust Disposal — 72 t sintering dust processed with curing agents.

Key Findings

  • Material feasibility confirmed: blending at 8:2 sludge-to-fly-ash compensates for chemical shortfalls relative to Riley's criteria.
  • Heavy metal immobilisation effective: sintering stabilises Cd, Cr, Cu, and Pb within the ceramic matrix above 1,000°C — transforming hazardous MSWI fly ash into a non-hazardous construction material.
  • Ceramsite unit requires ~4× less capital investment per tonne than a biogas digester (€34.49 vs. €111.70/tonne).
  • Net profit of €2.37/tonne is ~6× lower than biogas (€14.53/tonne), reflecting weaker market pricing and absence of renewable energy policy support.
  • Validated end-uses include bio-filter media (BAF), constructed wetland substrates, construction insulation, and soil conditioner.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that municipal sewage sludge combined with MSWI fly ash can be successfully converted into functional ceramsite through rotary kiln sintering, simultaneously addressing two hazardous waste streams while producing a commercially applicable material.

The ceramsite production unit requires ~4× less capital investment per tonne than a biogas digester, making it highly accessible for lower-capital contexts. Government policy and incentives are the decisive economic differentiator — biogas profitability is substantially driven by feed-in tariffs and renewable energy subsidies not available to ceramsite producers.

Key technical limitations requiring further research include bio-filter synergistic effects, Pb(II) adsorption performance, and robust flue gas purification for heavy metals and dioxins released during high-temperature pyrolysis. The project aligns with UN SDGs 6, 8, 9, 11, 13, 14, 15, and 17.
